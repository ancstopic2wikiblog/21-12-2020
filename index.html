History

How cockpit displays has evolved over the years

  • Before 1970s

    Analogue Display

    Analogue instruments usually got their information from the associated physical phenomena such as air pressure and airspeed. Instruments were arrays of dials and were generally designed for a three-man crew. In a typical cockpit, there were more than 100 instruments competing for the pilots information, these instruments were installed all across the various instrument panels.

  • After 1970s

    Digital: CRT Display

    Improvements in electronics and computer technology, meant that data could now be converted from analogue to digital format, processed by computers, and displayed on digital displays in the cockpit. This was first adopted on the Airbus A310, Boeing 757 and 767 and allowed for the instrument panel to be simplified significantly however, some instruments such as engine indicators were still using analog displays.

  • Mid 1990s - Present

    Digital: LCD Cockpit

    The development of LCD technology helped bring us into the glass cockpit era. LCD displays were favored by the manufacturers for their efficiency and reliability. They also allowed manufacturers to save weight as 1 display could be used for multiple purposes displaying different types of information. Used in most modern commercial aircraft such as the Airbus A350, Boeing 777 and Boeing 787

  • Future

    Touch-screen Display

    The future of cockpits will see the introduction of new technologies such as touchscreen displays. For example, on the Boeing 777x which is currently under development, touchscreens are being introduced to enable the pilot to interact with flight management system functions, tune the radios and adjust aircraft systems. With single touch and multi touch capability, it will allow the pilot to scroll a map, zoom and pan an image just like how we would do it on our phones currently.

State-Of-The-Art Cockpit Displays

Larger Displays

Cockpit of Irkut MS 21

When cockpits moved from analogue dials to digital displays to display information to the pilots, the main advantage was that information being displayed to the pilots was much clearer and more accurate. Instead of having analogue dials all over the cockpit and needing to search for the correct dial, the information was now displayed digitally on digital displays in front of the pilots.

Currently, most commercial airliners have 6 LCD displays. For example, on the Boeing 777 there are 2 for the PFD, 2 for the ND, 1 for the EICAS and 1 for the lower MFD.

However, the current trend with newer airliners that are entering into service is for the number of displays the size of each display to be increased and the number of displays in total to be reduced.

For example, on the modern Airbus A220 (previously known as the Bombardier CS100 or CS300) there are only 5 big LCD Displays.

The main advantages of having larger displays are that:
  • Allows information to be displayed more clearly
  • Allows the pilot to understand information quickly
  • Displays can be reconfigured based on which phase of flight the plane is at
  • Allows for the implementation of Synthetic Vision

Synthetic Vision

Garmin's G3000 system

Synthetic Vision System (SVS) also known as Synthetic Vision, is a type of computer system that uses 3D Imagery to provide pilots with a visual reference of the environment they are currently flying in.

It was originally developed back in the 1970s by NASA and the US Air Force, however the system was only installed on a Gulfstream V test aircraft in 2005 in partnership with NASA.

The system was only certified by the FAA at the end of 2007 and early 2008. The earliest versions of Synthetic Vision were installed on the Gulfstream G350, G450 and G550 aircraft. This version was capable of displaying 3D colour terrain images from an onboard database overlaid with the PFD symbology. This meant that the traditional artificial horizon was replaced. However, as technology has advanced more display system manufacturers such as Garmin and Rockwell Collins are able to integrate Synthetic Vision into their display systems.

The main advantages of Synthetic Vision are:
  • It wallows the pilot to navigate around terrain when the weather is less than ideal
  • It improves the situational awareness of the pilot

Head-Up Display

Cockpit of Boeing 737-800

A Heads-Up Display is one of the newer display technologies that can be found inside modern aircraft cockpits. The Heads-Up Display makes use of a transparent display that is placed within the line of sight of the pilot. The Heads-Up Display shows essential information that is identical to the Primary Flight Display to the pilot.

This technology was mainly developed for military uses; however, it is becoming increasingly common in commercial airliners and business jets. Some aircraft that currently have the Heads-Up Display installed in the cockpit are the Boeing 787 and Gulfstream G650.

The main advantage of having a Heads-Up Display installed is that the pilot does not need to change their eye focus to look at the PFD when they are flying the aircraft, this is especially useful during situations such as take-off and landing where concentration is of utmost importance.

New ways of interacting with the aircraft

QWERTY Keyboard

QWERTY Keyboard of Airbus A380

Arguably one of the most important pieces of equipment that can be found in the cockpit would the Flight Management System (FMS). It is through this system that the pilot would enter important pieces of information such as the flight plan and weight of the aircraft such that the aircraft would be able to follow the flight plan to reach its destination safely. In order to key in all this information, the pilot makes use of the Control Display Unit (CDU) which has a keyboard to enable the pilot to do so.

Currently, on older aircraft such as the Airbus A320, the keyboard is laid out in alphabetical order from âAâ to âZâ. This is currently the most common layout that can be found on most aircraft. However, as times have changed and typing on a computer has become a more common thing, on newer aircraft such as the Airbus A380 and A350, the keyboard layout has changed to the QWERTY layout that is similar to the layout on our computers.

The advantage of having a QWERTY keyboard layout compared to a keyboard laid out in alphabetical order is that the pilots are able to type in information in a quicker and more efficient manner as they are using a layout that is familiar and common to them.

Touch Screen Display

Gulfstream's Symmetry Flight Deck

As touchscreens have solidified their place in our lives, they are slowly also making their way into the cockpits of newer aircraft. Currently, the most common way to control the different systems onboard the aircraft is via switches or knobs that are mainly found on the overhead panel of the cockpit. However, in some aircraft such as the newly introduced Gulfstream G600 and Boeing 777X, touchscreen displays are being introduced as the main way of interacting with the aircraft.

For example, in the Gulfstream G600 most of the overhead panel has been condensed down to just 3 touchscreen displays to control the various systems on board the aircraft. Compared to the older Gulfstream G650 which still has a traditional overhead panel with knobs and switches, having a touchscreen display makes the cockpit feel more modern and less cramped with knobs and switches everywhere.

The main advantage of having touchscreen displays as a way of interacting with the aircraft is that it helps to save space as the overhead panel does not need to include all the knobs and switches everywhere. Another advantage of having touchscreen displays is that it is also a more intuitive way for the pilots to interact with the aircraft.

Gesture and Voice Control

Augmented Reality Glasses

As devices start to become more interconnected and the amount of computing power available to us has increased over the years, a proposed new way of interacting with the aircraft is with the use of gesture & voice controls. The current proposal would be for the pilot to wear an augmented reality headset or a pair of special glasses. The pilot would then be able to look at the instrument, select and manipulate it using voice commands.

For example, if the pilot would wish to change the squawk code on the transponder, he could point at the transponder and use the voice command âSet 1200â instead of having to manually tune the numbers. In addition to that, the augmented reality headset or special glasses can also be used to display information from the instrument to the pilots, which is similar to the Heads-Up Display.

Design Consideration

Display Design

Cockpit of Airbus A220-100

One of the main considerations when designing the cockpit is the design of the displays used in the cockpit. With regards to this, there are 3 different factors taken into consideration, mainly the use of analogue dials vs digital instruments, different colours used to present different types of information, and the ability for the display to display different warnings from the warning system.

The first factor, of analogue vs digital. Both analogue and digital have their own advantages, for example an analogue display would require minimal effort to interpret the information whereas for a digital display, the information is provided by providing plain numbers instead of having a needle on a scale. Digital displays also require lesser amount of space to display the same amount of information compared to an analogue instrument. When designing a cockpit this is one of the factors taken into consideration.

The second factor is the use of different colours to represent different types of information. When designing a cockpit, the different types of colours used all have different meaning and can represent different severity levels.

Some common types of colours used, and their meanings are as follows:
  • Green : Active Items
  • White : Present Status
  • Magenta : Command Information, Pointers, Symbols, and Fly to Tracks
  • Cyan : Non active or background information
  • Red : Warnings
  • Amber : Caution or Faults
  • Black : Blank Areas

The third factor taken into designing the cockpit is the integration of warning systems. The warning system on the aircraft serves 4 main purposes; to alert the crew of a problem, to describe the nature of the problem, to direct the appropriate actions to take to solve the problem, and to provide feedback to the crew. In general, onboard the aircraft failures can be split into 2 categories: Warnings (Red) or Cautions (Amber). Warnings indicate that action needs to be taken immediately by the crew or it will affect the safety of the flight. Cautions are to alert the crew of a problem; however no immediate action is required. Warnings can be either visual, aural or tactile.

Standardized Layout

Cockpit of Boeing 747-8

An important factor when it comes to designing the cockpit is the layout of the cockpit. Most manufacturers try to follow a standardized layout when it comes to designing the layout of the cockpit.

In most modern airliners, the layout of the displays is placed in the same order and most aircraft have the same layout with small changes depending on the manufacturer. In addition to that, switches are also generally placed in the same location from aircraft to aircraft to avoid confusion.

Some common types of colours used, and their meanings are as follows:
  • Instrument Panel: Arranged in front of the pilot below the front windshield, contains the Primary Flight Display, Navigation Display, and other instruments
  • Side Panels: At the side of the pilotsâ seats below the side windshield, contains the Electronic Flight Bag in some aircraft like the Boeing 787
  • Mode Control Panel: Placed above the instrument panel and below the front windshield between the 2 pilots, used to control the autopilot of the aircraft
  • Centre Instrument Panel: Positioned between the Captainâs and First Officerâs instrument panel, normally used to display the EICAS
  • Centre Pedestal: Positioned between the two pilotsâ seats, contains controls such as the throttle quadrant, radio panel, and transponder
  • Overhead Panel: Positioned above both pilots and contains the various switches to control the different systems of the aircraft.

Operation of Knobs and Switches

Airbus A320 Overhead Panel

As mentioned above, switches in the cockpit are generally placed in the same location from aircraft to aircraft to avoid confusion and to further assist the pilot. However, it is not only the location of the knobs and switches that are taken into consideration when designing the cockpit, but more importantly how the knobs and switches are being operated by the pilot.

When designing how knobs operate, the most important factor taken into account has to do with how the human logic works and how we want things to work in a natural sense. An example of this is in the cockpit is to control the brightness of the displays. When we turn the brightness knob to the right, we expect the brightness to increase. Having knobs and switches that operate in a natural sense will help to reduce the amount of confusion in the cockpit during an emergency situation for example where the stress levels are high.

Similarly, when designing how switches work certain manufacturers such as Airbus have adopted the âDark Cockpit Conceptâ. This concept refers to the way that switches and systems that are operating in a normal and serviceable manner will not be lighted up. Only when a fault occurs, then will a light turn on.

Ergonomics

Pilots and flight crew on a U.S. Air Force E-3G

The cockpit plays a very important role each time the plane takes to the skies, being the bridge between the aircraft and the crew, the cockpit of a modern airliner should allow the crew to assess the current status of the aircraft and take any appropriate actions in case of emergency in an instantaneous and convenient manner.

As a result of this, ergonomics plays a very important factor when deciding how a cockpit should be designed, the layout of the instrumentation and the operations of controls in the cockpit.

The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) states that the minimum conditions requirements that should be met when designing a cockpit are as follows:
  • The ability for the pilot to reach various controls without effort from his seat, with the seat belt attached and shoulder harness unlocked.
  • The pilot should be able to have clear visibility of the various flight instruments without needing to move
  • The pilot should be able to have good visibility outside of the cockpit
  • The pilots should be able to communicate orally easily.

In addition to that, the accessibility of the primary flight controls is very important as well.

Challenges Encountered

Digital Display

Modern cockpit display nowadays have become more digitised to reduce the pilotâs workload and display data more clearly to pilots. Despite this, there are also downsides of having such digital displays in the cockpit. Firstly, in a research conducted by the NTSB (National Transportation Safety Board), it concluded that fatal accidents are more prone to happening, in the research conducted by the NTSB, they concluded that pilots were spending more time learning the glass cockpit during training and less on the actual skill of flying. Secondly, it can be overwhelming for new pilots when they first fly an aircraft with digital displays because the displays are usually comprises of many different types of flight data being displayed on one screen, this would mean that it is more difficult for pilots to familiarize themselves with where the different readings are displayed on the display.

Technologies

Wearable technology will not develop in civil aviation for at least 10 to 15 years. Design of the display will be more complex as there are more information and controllers than a carâs digital dashboard. Advanced technology requires extensive amount of research & development (R&D) to develop the equipment. Resulting in a larger cost and longer time. Number of controllers (switches and buttons) and indicators (instrument and displays) that are separated from each other instead of being integrated. For example, to change the aircraft speed, pilot needs to access one control unit in that cockpit area and check the speed on another display in another cockpit area.

There are a few key questions that designers will face and fulfil the following requirements of designing a futuristic aircraft cockpit:
  • Meeting the aircraftâs certification and safety standard: Aircraft design will have to meet the specifications and the controllers are easily and safely access by the pilots.
  • Avoid mode confusion: Preventing any aircraft movements to not sync with the pilots controllers.
  • Simplicity: To make it simple for pilots to access important controllers for emergency situation
  • Messages and alerts: To make it more convenient and easy to understand what are the warnings and messages for the pilots to understand

Cost of Equipment

The cost of an future aircraft cockpit is not cheap due to the implementation of the advanced aircraft technology. One entire aircraft can wipe out about $15 billion cost advantage offered by a single-pilot cockpit. Meaning one pilot will be accessing the controllers in the cockpit and the other pilot will standby on ground to provide extra support by monitoring. Ideally the best scenario is to have two pilots in the cockpit.

Another consideration is the cost of the advance technology to all aircrafts. It is possible to apply high technology to some aircrafts, however it will take several years to achieve and standardized all aircrafts to have the same equipment and technologies.
